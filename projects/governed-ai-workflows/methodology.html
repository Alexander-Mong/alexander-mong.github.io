Governed AI Workflows

Most AI text analysis can’t be traced back. This one traces every finding it keeps to its source.

The usual approach hands a model a pile of text and asks it to summarize, and you have no way to know what it made up or what it left out. I work the other way around. What’s on display here isn’t a model; it’s the controls you’d put around an AI workflow before trusting its output: every finding tied to the exact words in its source, and anything that doesn’t match the source thrown out. Here that material is the advice given in startup and AI conference talks, about 62 hours across 153 transcripts.

Where the model decides — and where it doesn’t

A division of labor, so the model can only go wrong inside a boundary that ordinary software and a person can catch:

The two finders catch different things. On a five-transcript pilot (a slice, with the full corpus still ahead), the AI’s pass turned up about as many advice passages again as the rules had, nearly all phrased in ways the rules’ patterns couldn’t reach. Folding the two found more than either alone. Every kept item ties back to a verbatim quote you can check against the source in seconds.

The controls that test it

To test the AI’s judgment, I built a generator that throws plausible non-advice at it — a rule that grabs number- and quantity-spans, never real advice — and ran 122 of its items through the judge. The junk looked like this:

“they take about nine months to complete.”
“roughly a million tokens in its context limit.”

It accepted 3. Looking at them, the generator had accidentally grabbed real advice (e.g. “Don’t show someone 10 things and ask them to pick no more than three.”), so those 3 were arguably correct accepts of mislabeled junk, not misses; the set wasn’t perfectly clean, and I’d re-run it with the contaminants removed before leaning on the exact rate. The control I lean on harder is deterministic: every kept item has to quote the source word-for-word, and anything that didn’t match was dropped — about 6%, 45 items.

And the judge has to clear a bar I fixed before testing any model: reject at least 90% of a planted set of tricky non-advice. The bar is the control — anything I plug in has to pass it. The judge I’m using clears it; a candidate I tried didn’t, so it’s out.

691 advice items are in the set so far, drawn from the full corpus (not the five-transcript pilot above) — each tied to the exact passage it came from.

The hard part: is it actually useful?

Telling whether something is advice is easier than telling whether it’s any good:

Genuinely useful“the most underrated skill for founders is the skill to learn skills.”
Passes the filter, but empty“So don’t do that.” — grammatically advice; means nothing on its own.

So I tried to grade usefulness, and this is where it broke. The clearly-low-value items separated cleanly from the strong ones across repeated blind passes, but ranking the ambiguous middle didn’t hold: an early scoring step looked clean on a small run, then fell apart on a larger one. I cut it and kept only what the evidence supported — flag the clearly-low and clearly-high, leave the middle to a person. (Usefulness checks are from a sample.)

It’s mid-build, but the remaining work is scale-up, not invention: the full item-by-item human review and the usefulness ranking come next. What already holds is the spine — every kept finding tied to its source, a junk control the judge has to clear, and a result I pulled when the evidence turned against it. The findings are public and source-linked; the method is laid out here.
