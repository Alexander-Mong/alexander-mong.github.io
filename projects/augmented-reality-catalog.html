2013 Unity/Vuforia mobile AR sales catalog

Fence Experts / BOOST AR Catalog

I built the app-side Unity/Vuforia implementation and 3D product assets for a 2013 Fence Experts / BOOST AR sales catalog that turned printed brochure imagery into interactive mobile 3D fence/product views for sales conversations. It shipped before Apple's ARKit or Google's ARCore made phone-based AR mainstream.

The result was a sales-support product-visualization tool: a physical printed catalog could trigger interactive 3D fence views on a mobile device during customer conversations.

My work covered image-target setup, Unity scene and app flow, mobile UI behavior, touch/product interaction, and the 3D fence models and textures used in the app.

Problem / concept

Making the brochure interactive

The point was not AR for its own sake. The goal was to make the physical catalog more useful during a customer conversation. Instead of only showing fence photos and descriptions, the brochure could trigger interactive 3D product views on a phone or tablet.

This was image-target mobile AR work in 2013. The project depended on printed-image recognition, careful target selection, Unity content, and mobile interaction design.

Product interaction

After the catalog came alive

The AR overlay was the hook: point the device at the brochure, and the fence appeared on the page. From there, the app let users keep exploring instead of ending at a single effect.

Sales staff could open fence-family views, compare styles, and change visible options like top style, cap style, spacing, arrangement, and height. Those changes updated the model on screen, turning abstract product choices into something customers could see and discuss.

Role

My role

My work covered the app-side Unity/Vuforia implementation: image-target setup, scene flow, mobile UI behavior, touch/product interaction, and the 3D fence models and textures used in the app.

I connected the printed brochure to the interactive product experience: when the app recognized supported catalog imagery, it displayed the matching fence model and let users continue into product-viewing screens.

I also advised on which printed images would work well as AR targets. Catalog artwork, branding, logos, and print layout were handled separately by others. My responsibility was the coded Unity/Vuforia app experience and the 3D product assets.

Public launch

Public rollout

The project was built for a real sales/catalog context and tied to publicly distributed Fence Experts / BOOST materials. The augmented-reality brochure was publicly rolled out on Aug. 31, 2013.

The launch post introduced the BOOST phone/tablet experience, showed the physical brochure, and credited me for development work. That context anchors the project as a public client-facing AR catalog: printed sales material connected to a working Unity/Vuforia mobile product viewer.
